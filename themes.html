Themes

  • Life as an immigrant and the fake American dream
  • The family Sinclair uses to tell his story is a family of Lithuanian immigrants
  • They traveled to America do their belief that they would achieve the American dream, a dream where hard work and moral behavior would result in material success and supreme happiness
  • However once in America they are forced to work extremely difficult and unsatisfactory jobs for very low wages barely earning enough to get by
  • For many immigrants and Americans for that matter, the American dream is nothing more than a dream
  • The family discovers that to be successful in America, hard work and morals do not lead there, and that in fact corruption and crime are a much quicker way
  • The American dream has become a hollow idea of what it once was, as greed and excessive wealth has become the new American dream
  • Socialism as a possible cure for the problems presented by capitalism
  • Sinclair has a very negative view of capitalism, and he emphasizes it at the beginning of nearly every chapter
  • He views it as immoral, unjust, and even violent
  • The immigrants who traveled to America with dreams of success, are used up in this capitalistic society, nearly nothing more than slaves
  • The labor they partake in is brutal, and threatens their life and the well being of their families
  • Sinclair uses the protagonist of the story to express his belief that socialism is in fact the cure for the problems of capitalism
  • Sinclair spends much of the novel contrasting capitalism's flaws with socialism's strengths and urging readers that adopting a socialistic society would benefit all as opposed to benefiting few like capitalism does
